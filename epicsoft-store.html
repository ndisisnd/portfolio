Transforming boring e-commerce to interactive visuals

There's games to sell, there's people that'll buy it. The problem is that all around Asia, there are de facto e-commerce platforms that people already use. These e-commerce platforms are evolving every day with huge teams of UX designers that know what will convert every user into a customer. To launch into an oversaturated Taiwan market, we needed to make a huge splash.


I was part of this ambitious project to design, test, and ship complete designs within two months.

The Challenge

Hypothesising, Understanding, Designing and Shipping an Experience

With a plethora of e-commerce platforms, it's easy to find design patterns that are tried and tested.

My challenge was to innovate on this: how can I discover what makes an experience? What do people like about shopping that makes them keep coming back to a platform? I had many questions that needed answering, and the target users were in a foreign market that I have not interacted with before. The lack of cultural insight, easy access to resources, and communication barriers made the Empathy process a pain.

Pulling heads together

Understanding internal insights in Epicsoft

At Epicsoft, the business development team has worked extensively with the Taiwan and Hong Kong market, and thus hold many insights that may potentially be useful. I requested for a meeting and gathered the team, went over the specs, and asked for any internal insights that may help in this project. I ran a remote affinity mapping exercise on Figma and pieced together a mindmap.

Experience vs. E-commerce

It's more than e-commerce

It's easy to say "build something that's not e-commerce, but still e-commerce", but the fact is almost every e-commerce platform that's popular to the Taiwan and Hong Kong market looks like carbon copies or variants of Shopee and Lazada. Using the insights from my colleagues, I knew what I needed to go against the grain.

Building v0

Dark Mode e-commerce

Going along the lines of game-related platforms/websites such as cs.money, Discord, Twitch, and Steam, I hypothesised that going for dark mode would be the most ideal. These are mostly gamers that we're targeting: they usually browse at night with very minimal light, and having a dark mode design would suit their browsing style.

I took inspiration from other e-commerce platforms and incorporated the tried and tested designs patterns that they already have, and created the first iteration of OhMai after two weeks of prototyping.

Vehement Rejection

Entire designs scrapped

I had access to a pool of Taiwan users as one of our contacts who could make the project possible is based in Taipei. Utilising his network, I scheduled a few remote user tests and gathered their initial feedback from a pool of 25 people. This was followed by another test with internal stakeholders.

Unfortunately, the designs were scrapped. According to feedback, the website was "boring" and "liveless". I dove deeper into this and dug into why they considered the wesite boring. The lack of interactions, visual stimulus, and "shock factor" led me to understand that I needed to work on the visual design.

Change in Project Management style

From waterfall to agile

Previously, the directive always came from the executives, which funnelled down to our PM. The issue is that we treated each task as a blocker to another: I had to complete the overall product page design before working on a single product page. That's cool—but that led to the vehement rejection from before. We needed a way to work concurrently, and test things in parallel. Hence, the project manager decided to adopt an Agile methodology with the tech team and soon I was back on the familiar cycle of standups, retrospectives, and assigning tasks to myself.

Testing v0.5 in a sprint

Since they are gamers, why not make a game UI?

Inspired by gaming HUDs (Heads-up Display) that show character statistics and equipment, why not make a platform that's similar to that? Imagine an experience where users come on the platform and buy something for themselves as if they are in a game—they are the game character. With this, I decided to create a concept for the KOL storefront page and tested it with a new set of Taiwanese customers (who were previous customers of Play-E).

Some key screens from v0.5

Lukewarm response

One step closer for OhMai

It was an interesting concept, but the users had a few frustrations:

  • What was the point of this page? What am I doing here when I land on this page?
  • Where can I buy things on the KOL storefront?
  • How do I get to another section in the KOL storefront page?

I had my fair share of good feedback, which I used for the next sprint:

  • People liked vibrant colours that look active
  • Traditional, flat user interface design makes the website the same as everything else
  • People usually use the same sites over and over again

Conceptualising v1

Glass panels > Flat colours

Using glass panels were tough: how do I ensure that I can maintain accessibility and contrast while using glassmorphism as a base? I opted for a new background, and created a whole new design system surrounding this concept. I aimed to create an experience whilst balancing the purpose of the platform: users are here to purchase things. My objective was to not make them feel like they're here just for that.

Testing the concept for the KOL storefront

Testing v1 in a sprint

A whole new world

I tested the first storefront concept with the KOLs themselves. My objective was to answer these few questions:

  • Do they feel like it represented them?
  • Do they feel compelled to promote the page?
  • Do they feel that the storefront was something they would be proud to have?

With a pool of 7 different KOLs from different regions in Taiwan, I managed to gather enough feedback to iterate on the storefront. Subsequently, I iterated on the previous v0 designs and created a whole new set.

An arduous process in overhauling designs

Launching a new set of designs with new features

With a tight deadline of only three sprints, I had to forgo certain design decisions. Innovative UX had to be implemented carefully since there was not much time left to test with users, considering that the Design team is just myself. To manage this, I decided to segment out the modules that have "innovative UX" or less orthodox flows, then test a bunch of them over a few days with another set of customers.

The result? A whole series of designs brimming with energy of a new concept.

Post-Launch Reflections

A breathless execution

The extremely tight deadline made working on this project a huge breathless run. Each sprint felt like I was trying to achieve a triathlon in one day—thrice. I had to take into account many factors that were mostly uncontrollable: engineering constraints and business development concerns.

One thing I would do differently would be to lay out a design system very early on that was more modular. I was under a wrong impressiong that I could simply ship v0—and the horror came in when I had to overhaul the design system. That led to a lot of delays especially with tweaking colours, typography, and components throughout Figma.
